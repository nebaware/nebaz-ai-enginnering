Tutorial 2: How Does AI Work?

Understand the inner logic of AI systems — how they learn from data and make predictions

📅 Week 1 📊 Beginner

Imagine teaching a child to recognize dogs. You don't hand them a rulebook saying "four legs + fur + tail = dog." Instead, you show them hundreds of photos: big dogs, small dogs, cartoon dogs, even plush toys. After enough examples, something magical happens — the child stops memorizing and starts recognizing.

That's AI in a nutshell: learning from examples to spot patterns. No programmer sits down and writes "if tail wags, then happy dog." The machine figures it out by observing thousands of cases.

💡 Key Insight: AI doesn't follow instructions — it discovers patterns from data, then uses those patterns to make predictions about new, unseen information.

In this module, you'll use tools like ChatGPT and Teachable Machine to see AI learning in action—experiencing firsthand how machines recognize patterns and make predictions.

🧠 What AI Actually Does

At its core, AI works by:

  1. Processing data — analyzing examples (images, text, numbers)
  2. Finding patterns — detecting relationships and rules automatically
  3. Making predictions — applying learned patterns to new situations
  4. Improving over time — adjusting based on feedback

Unlike traditional software that follows pre-written rules, AI creates its own rules by observing data. It's less "programming" and more "teaching through examples."

👨‍🍳 The Chef Learning Analogy

Think of AI as a chef learning a recipe — not by reading instructions, but by tasting hundreds of dishes:

  • After 10 pasta dishes, the chef notices: "Ah, salt makes it pop!"
  • After 50 dishes: "More garlic = bolder flavor"
  • After 200 dishes: "This combination of herbs creates magic"

The chef never saw a recipe book. They learned by pattern recognition across hundreds of samples. That's exactly how AI models learn — by "tasting" data until patterns emerge.

✨ The Breakthrough: The chef can now create NEW dishes they've never tasted before, using principles learned from experience. That's prediction — applying learned patterns to novel situations.

📧 Real-World Example: Your Spam Filter

Your email spam filter is a perfect AI example. Here's what it does NOT do:

❌ Common Misconception: The spam filter doesn't have a list of "bad words" programmed in. It's not searching for "FREE MONEY" or "Click Here" like old-school filters did.

Instead, here's what actually happens:

Training Phase What the AI Learns
Fed millions of spam emails Strange sender domains, urgent language, suspicious links
Fed millions of safe emails Normal conversation patterns, known contacts, typical subjects
Compares patterns "Emails with X, Y, Z characteristics = 94% chance of spam"
Makes predictions New email arrives → AI calculates spam probability

The genius? The spam filter never "knows" what spam is — it just recognizes patterns statistically associated with junk mail. When scammers change tactics, the filter learns new patterns.

🎯 Hands-On Exercise: Train Your Own AI

Let's make this concrete. You're going to train an AI to recognize emotions in text — without writing a single line of code.

🎓 Goal: Teach an AI to distinguish between positive and negative sentences using Google's Teachable Machine.

Step-by-Step Instructions:

  1. Go to: teachablemachine.withgoogle.com
  2. Choose: "Text Project"
  3. Create two classes:
    • Class 1: "Positive"
    • Class 2: "Negative"
  4. Feed 10 examples each:
    • Positive: "I love this!", "Amazing experience!", "Best day ever!"
    • Negative: "This is terrible", "I'm so disappointed", "Worst purchase"
  5. Click "Train Model" (watch it learn patterns)
  6. Test it: Type "This made me smile" — does it guess correctly?

💡 What Just Happened? You didn't program rules. You provided examples. The AI found patterns like "love," "amazing," "terrible" correlating with sentiment. That's machine learning.

Extra Challenge:

Try confusing the AI with sarcasm: "Oh great, another Monday." Does it understand context? (Spoiler: probably not — that's a current limitation!)

⚠️ Common Mistakes About AI Learning

Mistake #1: "More Data = Smarter AI"

Reality: Quality beats quantity. 100 well-labeled examples outperform 10,000 messy, inconsistent ones. Bad data teaches bad patterns.

Mistake #2: "AI Understands Meaning"

Reality: AI doesn't "understand" anything. It recognizes statistical patterns. Your spam filter doesn't know what "money" means — it just sees that word appears in 73% of spam emails.

Mistake #3: "Untested Models Are Ready"

Reality: Without feedback and testing, AI stays naive. That's why your Netflix recommendations improve over time — it's learning from your viewing behavior.

📝 Mini-Project: Movie Sentiment Predictor

Let's apply what you've learned in a practical project you can do right now:

🎬 Build a Movie Review Classifier

Tools needed: Google Sheets + ChatGPT (or Gemini)

Instructions:

  1. Create a spreadsheet with 2 columns:
    • Column A: Movie Review (text)
    • Column B: Sentiment (Good/Bad)
  2. Add 20 reviews (10 positive, 10 negative)
  3. Ask ChatGPT: "Based on these reviews, predict sentiment for: [new review]"
  4. Compare AI predictions to your own judgments
  5. Track accuracy: how many did it get right?

What you'll discover: The AI identifies patterns you might not consciously notice — like "but" often signals a negative turn, or "exceeded expectations" indicates satisfaction.

💡 Reflection Questions: What patterns did the AI catch that you missed? Where did it fail? (Hint: sarcasm, cultural references, and subtle context often trip up AI.)

📚 Summary: The AI Learning Process

Let's recap what we've learned about how AI works:

  • ✅ AI learns from examples, not explicit programming
  • ✅ Pattern recognition is the core mechanism
  • ✅ Quality data matters more than quantity
  • ✅ Feedback loops help AI improve over time
  • ✅ AI doesn't understand — it detects statistical correlations

🎯 Key Takeaway: AI is organized guesswork, refined through feedback. It's not magic — it's math detecting patterns in data, then using those patterns to predict outcomes.
